\section{Preliminary Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Search Space Reduction}

We ran the search space analysis on the first four Nguyen benchmarks
(single-variable polynomial expressions) with $N = 500$ random strings
of up to 12 tokens each. Table~\ref{tab:reduction} shows the results.

\midrule
Nguyen-1 & 191 & 168 & 1.1$\times$ & 3.7 \\
Nguyen-2 & 194 & 168 & 1.2$\times$ & 3.6 \\

\textbf{Note}: These are preliminary results from a small local run
($N=200$, max 8 tokens). The modest reduction factors (1.1--1.2$\times$)
are expected at this scale because short strings produce few equivalent forms.
Full-scale experiments ($N=10{,}000$+, longer strings, all 12 Nguyen +
10 Feynman benchmarks, 30 independent runs) are expected to show
significantly higher reduction factors approaching $k!$ as string length
and $k$ increase.

\subsection{Observations}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Reduction is consistent}: Even with small random strings
    ($\leq 12$ tokens, $\bar{k} \approx 2$), canonicalization eliminates
    roughly $3\times$ redundant representations.
  \item \textbf{Reduction grows with $k$}: As expressions become more complex
    (more internal nodes), the reduction factor is expected to approach $k!$.
    This will be validated in the full experiments.
  \item \textbf{Deduplication is free}: The canonical string computation adds
    overhead per expression, but duplicate elimination saves evaluation costs
    (the dominant cost in SR).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Round-Trip Verification}

All 25 deterministic round-trip tests and 100+ Hypothesis property tests
confirm: $\text{S2D}(\text{D2S}(D)) \cong D$ for every tested DAG.
The canonical string is idempotent:
$\text{canonical}(\text{S2D}(\text{canonical}(D))) = \text{canonical}(D)$.

\subsection{Numerical Evaluation Preservation}

Evaluation is preserved across canonicalization: for all tested expressions
and inputs, $\text{eval}(D, x) = \text{eval}(\text{S2D}(\text{canonical}(D)), x)$
within floating-point tolerance. This ensures that the canonical representation
does not alter the mathematical semantics of the expression.
